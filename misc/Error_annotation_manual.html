1. Introduction

This manual is an adapted version of The Louvain Error Tagging Manual Version 2.0, expanded to include five additional error tags. It provides comprehensive guidelines for annotating errors in learner-produced texts, with each tag designed to identify the error type and suggest an appropriate correction.

3. Error Categories

3.1. Digitally-Mediated Communication

Capitalization: <DMCC>

Use <DMCC> for all instances of missing capitalization. These may include:

  • Uncapitalized first person singular pronoun "I".
    • Example: 
      <DMCC corr="I">i</DMCC> ate the famous "paella".
  • Uncapitalized word at the beginning of a sentence (after full stop, exclamation or question mark).
    • Example:
      As you think it's the best. <DMCC corr="We">we</DMCC> trust your taste
  • Uncapitalized word at at the beginning of the conversational turn.
    • Example: 
      <DMCC corr="Hi">hi</DMCC> there, thank you for asking me.
  • Uncapitalized proper nouns, or brand names.
    • Example: 
       I remember that they were from <DMCC corr="Nike">nike</DMCC>
  • Uncapitalized weekdays, months, holidays, languagues or nationalities:
    • Examples: 
       I have lessons from <DMCC corr="Tuesday">tuesday</DMCC> to <DMCC corr="Friday">friday</DMCC>
       I need to find a <DMCC corr="Christmas">christmas</DMCC> present

üí° Tip: If a word is capitalized even though it should not be, or if it is written in all caps, leave it untagged.

Abbreviations: <DMCA>

Use <DMCA> for abbreviations typically used in the digitally-mediated communication (e.g., OK, lol, OMG, etc.).

üí° Tip: If the abbreviation does not have a direct or meaningful equivalent in formal language, leave the corr attribute blank.

  • Example:
    Sometimes I play football with my friends, but I'm not so good <DMCA corr="">lol</DMCA>

3.2. Form

Spelling: <FS>

Use <FS> for all spelling errors, including:

  • Issues concerning the doubling of consonants/vowels.
  • Letter substitution or misordering.
  • Misuse/omission of hyphens/blanks between words or parts of words.
  • Homophones (it‚Äôs/its, their/there, etc.).
  • Missing apostrophe in verb abbreviations (Im, youre, etc.).
  • Typos.
    • Example: 
      <FS corr="Perfect">hperfect</FS>

    • üí° Tip: Never tag capitalization issues as <FS>.

    üí° Tip: Unless they actually contain a spelling error, do NOT tag as <FS>:

    1. Words in all caps (e.g.; HI).
      • ‚úÖ Correct: No tag needed for HI.
    2. Words ending with repeated letters (e.g., helppp).
      • ‚úÖ Correct: No tag needed for helppp.
      • ‚úÖ Correct: <FS corr="help">hielppp</FS>

    Morphology: <FM>

    Use <FM> for morphological errors on DERIVATIONAL affixes ONLY.

    Example: 

    <FM corr="impossible">unpossible</FM>

    3.3. Punctuation

    üí° Tip: Do NOT use punctuation tags for:

    1. Multiple punctuation marks (e.g., !!!).
      • ‚úÖ Correct: Leave untagged.
    2. Ellipses with fewer or more than three dots (e.g., .. OR ....).
      • ‚úÖ Correct: Leave untagged.
    3. Missing full stops at the end of a conversational turn.
      • ‚úÖ Correct: Leave untagged.
    4. Cases of comma splices (e.g., She said sorry, she doesn't want to scream like that ).
      • ‚úÖ Correct: Leave untagged.

    Example: 

    Where<QC corr="?">!</QC>

    Example:

    Punctuation Missing: <QM>

    Punctuation Redundant: <QR>

    3.4. Grammar

    Grammar: Determiners

    Demonstrative Determiners: <GDD>

    Use <GDD> for errors involving demonstrative determiners.

    Example: 

    What should I know about <GDD corr="these">those</GDD> subjects?

    Possessive Determiners: <GDO>

    Use <GDO> for errors involving possessive determiners.

    Example: 

    I like psychological horror films that play with <GDO corr="your">our</GDO> mind

    Indefinite Determiners: <GDI>

    Use <GDI> for errors involving indefinite determiners (e.g., any, some).

    Example: 

    I don't have <GDI corr="any"> some</GDI> friends.

    Other Determiners: <GDT>

    Use <GDT> for errors involving other types of determiners, such as:

    • Relative determiners (e.g., whose).
    • Interrogative determiners (e.g., which, what).

    üí° Tip: Use <GDT> only for errors not covered under <GDD>, <GDO> or <GDI>.

    Articles: <GA>

    Use <GA> for all problems of definite, indefinite or zero articles.

    Example: 

    I like <GA corr="\0">the</GA> green tea.

    Grammar: Adjectives

    Comparative/Superlative Adjectives: <GADJCS>

    Use <GADJCS> for wrong formation and/or misuse of comparatives and superlatives.

    Example: 

    I'm studying Criminology, it is <GADJCS corr="very">most</GADJCS> difficult.

    Adjective Number: <GADJN>

    Use <GADJN> for erroneous uses of plural morphemes with adjectives.

    Example: 

    the <GADJN corr="poor">poors</GADJN> people

    Adjective Order: <GADJO>

    Use <GADJO> for:

    • Problems of adjective position.
    • Word-order errors when two or more adjectives precede the noun.
    • Errors concerning adjectives with different meaning when used attributively or predicatively.
      • Example: 
        the <GADJO corr="people concerned">concerned people</GADJO> are asked to fill in a form

    Adverb Order: <GADVO>

    Use <GADVO> to tag misplaced adverbs.

    Example: 

    we <GADVO corr="also explored">explored also</GADVO> the centre of Corf√π town

    Grammar: Nouns

    Noun Case: <GNC>

    Use <GNC> for:

    • Inappropriate use of the Saxon genitive.
      • Example: 
        <GNC corr="Tower of London">London's Tower</GNC>
    • Use of a phrase where there should be a Saxon genitive.
      • Example: 
        <GNC corr="my favourite team's games">the games of my favourite team</GNC>
    • Omission of the genitive marker.
      • Example: 
        <GNC corr="beginner's guide">beginners guide</GNC>

    Noun Number: <GNN>

    Use <GNN> for:

    • Singular for plural (and vice versa).
      • Example: 
        What do you think about the 3 <GNN corr="losses">loss</GNN> of the Yankees?
    • Errors in irregular plurals.
      • Example: 
        <GNN corr="mice">mouses</GNN>
    • Use of genitive marker instead of plural -s.
      • Example: 
        underprivileged <GNN corr="beings">being's</GNN>

    Grammar: Pronouns

    Demonstrative Pronouns: <GPD>

    Use <GPD> to tag errors involving demonstrative pronouns.

    Example: 

    if <GPD corr="that">\0</GPD> is comfortable with you

    Personal Pronouns: <GPP>

    Use <GPP> for errors involving personal pronouns.

    Example: 

    I didn't like <GPP corr="it">\0</GPP> so much

    Possessive Pronouns: <GPO>

    Use <GPO> for errors in possessive pronouns.

    Example: 

     she is <GPO corr="my">mine</GPO> sister

    Indefinite Pronouns: <GPI>

    Use <GPI> for ALL errors involving indefinite pronouns, including one/ones and the pro-form so.

    Example: 

    Could you please give me another solution, maybe a quicker <GPI corr="one">\0</GPI>?

    Reflexive/Reciprocal Pronouns: <GPF>

    Use <GPF> for errors in reflexive pronouns (myself, himself) or reciprocal pronouns (each other, one another).

    Example: 

    I respect <GPF corr="myself">me</GPF>.

    Relative/Interrogative Pronouns: <GPR>

    Use <GPR> for errors involving relative pronouns (who, which, that) or interrogative pronouns (who, what, where).

    Example: 

    I am a language trained model <GPR corr="which">who</GPR> can simulate human intelligence.

    Unclear pronominal reference: <GPU>

    Use <GPU> for cases where the referent of the pronoun is unclear.

    Example: 

    They never had a fair trial and a real chance to get out of <GPU corr="jail">it</GPU>.

    Grammar: Verbs

    Verb Auxiliaries: <GVAUX>

    Use <GVAUX> to tag:

    • Misuse of primary auxiliaries (be, have and do), e.g., in interrogative sentences.
      • Example: 
        <GVAUX corr="Do">\0</GVAUX> You have other recipes for gluten-free and vegan food?
    • Misuse of modal auxiliaries (EXCEPT shall and will which are tagged <GVT>).
    • Misuse of the semi-auxiliaries (dare, need, ought to and used to).
    • Missing auxiliary be in present or past continuous tense.
      • Example: 
        I <GVAUX corr="am">\0</GVAUX> eating pasta.

    Verb Morphology: <GVM>

    Use <GVM> for erroneous uses of verb morphology, i.e. misuse of inflectional verbal morphemes, including:

    • Use of simple past form instead of a past participle form (and vice versa).
      • Example: 
        I have <GVM corr="swum">swam</GVM> for two hours.
    • Erroneously formed 3rd person singular morphemes.
      • Example: 
        He <GVM corr="watches">watchs</GVM> television all day long.
    • Erroneous irregular past tense verb.
      • Example: 
        I have <GVM corr="swum">swimmed</GVM> for two hours.
    • Use of a non -ing verb form in present or past continuous tense.
      • Example: 
        I <GVM corr="'m staying">'m stay</GVM> with my family and my animals
    • Non-use of base form where required (e.g., in interrogative sentences, with modal verbs, etc.).
      • Example: 
        What do you <GVM corr="think">thinking</GVM> about this?

    Verb Number: <GVN>

    Use the <GVN> tag for errors where the verb form does not match the subject's number (singular or plural).

    Example: 

    a person that <GVN corr="wants">want</GVN> to go to Florence

    Verb Non-Finite/Finite: <GVNF>

    Use <GVNF> to tag:

    • -ing form used instead of a relative clause (and vice versa).
      • Example: 
        Hardly anybody from our district <GVNF corr="who is older">being older</GVNF> than fifteen goes to school regularly.
    • Nominal use of the bare infinitive instead of -ing form.
      • Example: 
        <GVNF corr="Booking">Book</GVNF> a table at the restaurant?
    • Nominal use of the infinitive with to instead of -ing form.
      • Example: 
        My favorite way to spend time in the weekend is <GVNF corr="relaxing">to relax</GVNF> at home with my boyfriend

    Verb Tense: <GVT>

    Use <GVT> to tag any misuse of tense or aspect, including:

    • Present simple for continuous (and vice versa).
    • Present simple for present perfect (and vice versa).
    • Present simple for simple past (and vice versa).
    • Simple past for present perfect (and vice versa).
    • Present simple for future (and vice versa).
    • Misuse of shall and will.
      • Example: 
        I <GVT corr="would">will</GVT> feel more secure if I could even control the quality of the water

    üí° Tip: If you change one verb‚Äôs tense, check and fix others in the same sentence.

    Verb Voice: <GVV>

    Use the <GVV> tag for errors involving the incorrect use of active or passive voice. This includes cases where active voice is used instead of passive, or vice versa.

    Example: 

    It is to <GVV corr="be hoped">hope</GVV> that‚Ä¶

    Grammar Word Class: <GWC>

    Use <GWC> to tag errors due to confusion between word classes (e.g., an adjective used instead of a noun or an adverb instead of an adjective).

    Example: 

    my friend is <GWC corr="dead">died</GWC>

    3.5. Lexico-Grammar

    Complementation

    Use the tags <XADJCO>, <XNCO>, <XPRCO> and <XVCO> for complementation errors, i.e. erroneous use of the finite and non-finite patterns that complement adjectives, adverbs, nouns, prepositions and verbs.

    Adjective Complementation: <XADJCO>

    Use <XADJCO> for errors in complementation patterns linked to adjectives.

    Example: 

    I am very <XADJCO corr="interested in visiting">interested in visit</XADJCO> famous medieval monuments

    Noun Complementation: <XNCO>

    Use <XNCO> for errors in complementation patterns linked to nouns.

    Example: 

    students have the <XNCO corr="possibility of leaving">possibility to leave</XNCO>

    Preposition Complementation: <XPRCO>

    Use <XPRCO> for errors in complementation patterns linked to pronouns.

    Example: 

    <XPRCO corr="instead of going">instead of to go</XPRCO> abroad

    Verb Complementation: <XVCO>

    Use <XVCO> for errors in complementation patterns linked to verbs.

    Example: 

    I really <XVCO corr="liked working">liked worked</XVCO> there

    Dependent Prepositions

    Use the tags <XADJPR>, <XADVPR>, <XNPR> and <XVPR> for errors involving prepositions that are intrinsically linked to a particular adjective, adverb, noun or verb.

    üí° Tips:

    1. Use dependent preposition tags also for cases of omission of a dependent preposition.
      • ‚úÖ Correct: <XADJPR corr="interested in">interested</XADJPR>
      • ‚ùå Incorrect: interested <XADJPR corr="in">\0</XADJPR>
    2. In the case of errors involving independent prepositions, use <LSPR>.
      • ‚úÖ Correct: I walked <LSPR corr='into'>in</LSPR> the room.

    Adjective Dependent Preposition: <XADJPR>

    Use <XADJPR> for errors involving prepositions required by adjectives.

    Example: 

    I'm <XADJPR corr="interested in">interested</XADJPR> a vacation in Italy

    Adverb Dependent Preposition: <XADVPR>

    Use <XADVPR> for errors involving prepositions required by adverbs.

    Example: 

    The investigators acted <XADVPR> corr="independently of">independently from</XADVPR> each other.

    Noun Dependent Preposition: <XNPR>

    Use <XNPR> for errors involving prepositions required by nouns.

    Example: 

    I have no <XNPR corr="idea of">idea to</XNPR> the basic role of materials

    Verb Dependent Preposition: <XVPR>

    Use <XVPR> for errors involving prepositions required by verbs.

    Example: 

    Can you <XVPR corr="explain to">explain</XVPR> me the situation?

    Uncountable/Countable Nouns: <XNUC>

    Use <XNUC> for:

    • Indefinite article with uncountable nouns.
      • Example: 
        I need to ask you for <XNUC corr="some information">an information</XNUC>
    • Plural of uncountable nouns.
      • Example: 
        I will take your <XNUC corr="advice">advices</XNUC>

    3.6. Lexis

    Lexis: Connectors

    Use the tags <LCC>, <LCS>, <LCLS> and <LCLC> for errors involving connectors, including:

    • Unnecessary use of a connector.
    • Omission of a necessary connector.
    • Wrong choice of connector.

    üí° Tip: If an erroneous preposition is part of a connector, use:

    • <LCLS> for simple connectors.
    • <LCLC> for multi-word or complex connectors.

    Coordinating Conjunctions: <LCC>

    Use <LCC> for errors in coordinating conjunctions (e.g., and, but, or).

    Example: 

    Now I would like to talk with you <LCC corr="and">\0</LCC> do a role play.

    Subordinating Conjunctions: <LCS>

    Use <LCS> for errors in subordinating conjunctions (e.g., because, although, while).

    Example: 

    <LCS corr="\0">because</LCS> I am very tired, how are you?

    Single Logical Connectors: <LCLS>

    Use <LCLS> for errors in single logical connectors (e.g., then, after, therefore).

    Example: 

    <LCLS corr="Then">After</LCLS> I would like an ice cream

    Complex Logical Connectors: <LCLC>

    Use <LCLC> for errors in multi-word or complex logical connectors (e.g., on the contrary, as a result).

    Example: 

    <LCLC corr="On the contrary">By the contrary</LCLC> not all improvements have been as positive as the ones previously said.

    Use the tags <LSADJ>, <LSADV>, <LSN>, <LSPR> and <LSV> for conceptual and collocational lexical errors in single words. These include solid and hyphenated compounds.

    üí° Tip: Use these tags also if the learner used a word that exists in English but whose meaning in the context is unclear. If you cannot guess at the meaning of a word, set corr="?".

    • Example: 
      I like <LSN corr="?">transportation</LSN> in Paris

    Single Adjectives: <LSADJ>

    Use <LSADJ> for errors involving adjective choices.

    Example: 

    <LSADJ corr="traditional">normal</LSADJ> plastic

    Single Adverbs: <LSADV>

    Use <LSADV> for errors involving adverb choices.

    Example: 

     she runs <LSADV corr="fast">rapidly<\LSADV>

    Single Nouns: <LSN>

    Use <LSN> for errors involving noun choices.

    Example: 

    it was a long <LSN corr="travel">trip</LSN>.

    Single Prepositions: <LSPR>

    Use <LSPR> for errors involving preposition choices.

    Example: 

    watching a film <LSPR corr="on">in</LSPR> the sofa

    Single Verbs: <LSV>

    Use <LSV> for errors involving verb choices.

    Example: 

    the work will be <LSV corr="finished">ended</LSV> in 10 days

    Lexical Phrases: <LP>

    Use <LP> the lexical error affects fixed word combinations, including:

    • Idioms.
      • Example: 
        it was a matter of hours before <LP corr="I was back to square one">I was back to where I started</LP>
    • (Unhyphenated) compound units.

    üí° Tip: Tag grammatical errors within set phrases under a single <LP> tag:

    • ‚ùå Incorrect: <LP corr="return the favour">recharge <GA corr="the">a</GA> favour</LP>

    Word Coinage/Calque: <LWCO>

    Use <LWCO> for:

    • Word and multi-word coinages and/or calques.
      • Example:

    3.7. Word:

    Word Missing: <WM>

    Use <WM> to tag omitted words. However, do NOT use <WM> for missing:

    • Articles: Use <GA>.
    • Connectors: Use <LCC>, <LCS>, <LCLS>, or <LCLC>.
    • Determiners: Use <GDD>, <GDO>, <GDI>, or <GDT>.
    • Pronouns: Use <GPD>, <GPP>, <GPO>, <GPI>, <GPF>, or <GPR>.
    • Dependent prepositions: Use <XADJPR>, <XADVPR>, <XNPR>, or <XVPR>.
    • Auxiliaries: Use <GVAUX>. (see Handling Missing and Redundant Elements).

    General Case Example: 

    Is it possible <WM corr="to">\0</WM> go by bus from the centre of Florence?

    Word Redundant: <WR>

    Use <WR> to tag unnecessary words. However, do NOT use <WR> for redundant:

    • Articles: Use <GA>.
    • Connectors: Use <LCC>, <LCS>, <LCLS>, or <LCLC>.
    • Determiners: Use <GDD>, <GDO>, <GDI>, or <GDT>.
    • Pronouns: Use <GPD>, <GPP>, <GPO>, <GPI>, <GPF>, or <GPR>.
    • Dependent prepositions: Use <XADJPR>, <XADVPR>, <XNPR>, or <XVPR>.
    • Auxiliaries: Use <GVAUX>. (see Handling Missing and Redundant Elements).

    General Case Example: 

     I <WR corr="\0">I</WR> loved the interpretation of Christian Bale

    Word Order: <WO>

    Use <WO> for problems of word order that do NOT fall into these categories:

    • Adverb Order <GADVO>
    • Adjective Order <GADJO>.

    Example: 

    <WO corr="I visited the Louvre last year">I visited last year the Louvre</WO>

    üí° Tip: In complex sentences with multiple errors, follow this approach:

    • First, tag other error categories (e.g., <GADVO, <FS>)

    Use <Z> to tag:

    • Stylistic problems: foreign-sounding or clumsy chunks of text that do NOT contain any errors as such and that therefore may require a reformulation.

    • Contractions.
    • Numbers written in numerals.

    • Connector issues.
    • Punctuation anomalies.

    3.9. Code-Switching

    üí° Tip: Do NOT use <CSINTRA> or <CSINTER> for:

    • Loanwords.
    • Proper nouns in another language.
    • Commonly integrated phrases.

    Intra-sentential: <CSINTRA>

    Use <CSINTRA> for language shifts within a sentence.

    Example: 

    I worked in a <CSINTRA corr="nursery school">asilo nido</CSINTRA>.

    Use <CSINTER> for language shifts between sentences or conversational turns.
